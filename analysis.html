THE LANGUAGE OF CONFLICT INTELLIGENCE

The Tacitus Ontology is a structural language for conflict and pre-conflict systems. It underpins everything Tacitus does: continuous monitoring of disputes, mapping trajectories of polarization, and generating resolution pathways instead of static reports.

01. RESOLUTION GAP

WHY A CONFLICT ONTOLOGY IS NECESSARY

STRUCTURAL PROBLEM

Modern institutions live inside permanent turbulence: email chains, board minutes, legal memos, social media cycles, diplomatic cables. The volume of conflictual information grows faster than any team’s ability to follow its structure. That mismatch creates a Resolution Gap: we detect more friction and polarization than we can systematically resolve.

Tacitus addresses this by treating every conflict or pre-conflict situation as a system of interacting actors, interests, constraints, and trajectories of polarization. The aim is continuous, automated understanding: who can move what, at what cost, and how today’s decisions change tomorrow’s risk of escalation or breakdown.

Continuous Understanding LIVE MODEL

The ontology feeds a live model of conflicts and pre-conflict tensions, updated as new information and signals arrive rather than frozen in episodic reports.

Automated Monitoring EARLY WARNING

Tacitus monitors polarization trajectories, veto paths and coalition fragility, surfacing where attention, mediation and guarantees are most urgent.

Resolution Technologies ACTIONABLE

The same structural layer powers conflict intelligence dashboards, Prism Lab polarization analysis and ontology graphs for scenario testing and resolution design.

02. TACITUS ONTOLOGY

STRUCTURAL RULES OF ENGAGEMENT // v3.3

ONTOLOGY STABLE

>> CIRCUITS OF POWER, NOT HEROES AND VILLAINS

The ontology sits at the intersection of systems thinking (Norbert Wiener, David Easton), strategic interaction (Schelling, Nash, Putnam), and institutional analysis (Tsebelis, Bueno de Mesquita, Ostrom), informed by political and moral philosophy (Arendt, Berlin, Habermas). It assumes that outcomes emerge less from the psychology of leaders than from the geometry of constraints and incentives in which they operate.

Every conflict system is modeled as a circuit between six fundamental structural roles:

INC

Incumbents

Actors or coalitions currently occupying the center: executives, ruling coalitions, dominant factions. Anchored in survival and maintenance logics, defending the status quo infrastructure.

CHL

Challengers

Reformers, opposition blocs, insurgent teams. They seek to rearrange the topology of power, often with higher risk tolerance and fewer institutional protections.

V

Veto Players

Any actor whose consent is required to alter the status quo. The number, distance and cohesion of veto players determine whether change is easy, difficult, or structurally impossible.

S / W

Selectorates

Derived from selectorate theory: broad constituencies (S) and the smaller winning coalitions (W) essential to survival. Tacitus tracks which coalitions are stable, brittle or shifting.

K / D

Kinetic & Discursive Planes

Conflict unfolds on two orthogonal planes: physical capability (kinetic) and narrative legitimacy (discursive). Shocks in one plane reprice risk in the other.

Ω

Win-Set Geometry

The mathematical intersection of outcomes acceptable to all decisive veto players. When the overlap shrinks, tensions rise; when it collapses, pre-conflict begins.

Tacitus makes the structure visible: who must move, who must be reassured, who can credibly guarantee, and which sequences of steps could re-open a viable Win-Set.

03. METRICS LAYER

POWER, RISK & POLARIZATION TRAJECTORIES

METRICS ONLINE
Power Metric NODE MASS

Structural capacity to force or block change: formal authority, control of chokepoints, agenda-setting power, or capacity to impose costs on others.

Risk Metric GLOW / HEAT

Exposure to adverse outcomes: reputational collapse, loss of mandate, sanctions, economic shocks, internal splits or legal liability.

Coherence Metric INTERNAL FRICTION

Measures how internally aligned a node or coalition is. Fragmented actors speak with many voices, weakening promises and making agreements fragile in practice.

Trajectory Metric POLARIZATION ARC

Tracks whether disagreements are stabilizing, oscillating, or hardening into identity-bound conflict. This is the early-warning layer for pre-conflict.

Conflict Intelligence Engine

Uses power and risk metrics to highlight pivotal actors and pressure points in active disputes, and to simulate how different decisions change exposure over time.

Prism Lab

Leans on trajectory and coherence metrics to map polarization arcs: which coalitions are radicalizing, which moderating nodes are losing centrality, and where narrative reframing may still work.

Ontology Graph

Visualizes the same metrics across domains, allowing teams to see at a glance where power is concentrated, where risk is underpriced, and which structural reforms (new guarantees, procedures, channels) would most change the topology.

04. VECTOR ANATOMY

HOW POWER FLOWS THROUGH THE GRAPH

EDGE TYPES

>> HARD POWER VECTORS

  • VETO
    Legal or procedural kill switch: board votes, constitutional courts, treaty vetoes. Highest-value edge; overuse signals institutional sclerosis.
  • CONFLICT
    Strikes, blockades, lawsuits, kinetic force: moments where bargaining collapses into damage and actors accept material loss to defend status or identity.
  • FUNDING
    Control of capital flows, budgets, grants: determines which projects survive and whose priorities are structurally subsidized.
  • DEPENDENCY
    Structural reliance on energy, logistics, supply chains, data platforms, or key personnel. Often invisible until it fails.
  • GUARANTEE
    Security, political, or economic guarantees that make agreements credible over time: insurance, monitoring regimes, escrow rules, safe seats, face-saving language.

>> SOFT POWER VECTORS

  • NARRATIVE
    Frames, stories, leaks, symbols and metaphors that confer or strip legitimacy. Narratives decide which losses are tolerable and which are unthinkable.
  • PRESSURE
    Protest, boycotts, campaigns, naming-and-shaming that raise the cost of inaction without direct force.
  • ALLIANCE
    Formal or informal pacts aggregating power; often create escalation tripwires and complicate de-escalation.
  • PROXY
    Indirect engagement via intermediaries to lower visible risk while still shaping outcomes: intermediating militias, consultants, shell firms, or front organizations.
  • MEDIATION CHANNEL
    Structured pathways where trust and confidentiality are sufficient for reframing and exploring concessions without immediate public cost.
05. ACTOR TAXONOMY

WHO OCCUPIES THE GRAPH

NODE TYPES

From a systems perspective, conflicts and trajectories of polarization emerge from the interaction of different families of actors, each with distinct mandates, resources, and vulnerabilities. The Tacitus actor ontology draws on comparative politics, IR and governance research (Tsebelis, Bueno de Mesquita, Ostrom, Keck & Sikkink, Haas and others) to capture those roles in a way that is portable across cases and levels.

A single real-world organization can occupy more than one role (for example, a government can be Executive, Funder and Security at once). Tacitus, however, treats these as separate node types, so that power, risk and polarization can be analyzed in terms of functions, not just institutional logos.

1. Governance & Authority

These actors define and enforce the formal “rules of the game” and are central to most selectorate and veto-player configurations.

E — EXECUTIVE
Central authority: governments, CEOs, executive boards, secretariats. Anchored in survival and continuity; highly sensitive to selectorates and legitimacy crises.

L — LEGISLATURE / ASSEMBLY
Parliaments, councils, shareholder meetings. Convert societal conflicts into formal decisions: budgets, laws, resolutions. Often collective veto players with internal factions.

R — REGULATOR / JUDICIARY
Courts, commissions, supervisory authorities, central banks. Translate conflicts into compliance and legal thresholds; can freeze or re-route entire trajectories with a single ruling.

O — OVERSIGHT & ACCOUNTABILITY
Audit institutions, ombuds, inspectors general, national human rights institutions, ethics bodies. Guardians of integrity and reputation; slow to act but catalytic when they do.

P — LOCAL / SUBNATIONAL AUTHORITY
Municipalities, regional governments, site managers, local councils. Carry granular knowledge and often face the first wave of discontent when national decisions bite.

I — INTERNATIONAL / MULTILATERAL
Intergovernmental organizations, missions and treaty bodies. Aggregate and constrain state behaviour; provide forums, monitoring, and sometimes enforcement or guarantees.

2. Coercive & Disruptive Power

Actors whose leverage rests on the ability to deploy, withhold or credibly threaten physical force, disruption or withdrawal of cooperation.

K — SECURITY / MILITARY / POLICE
Armed forces, police, intelligence, private security. Organized coercive apparatus with doctrines and rules of engagement; latent veto players in fragile systems.

A — ARMED NON-STATE ACTOR
Rebel groups, militias, criminal cartels, paramilitaries. Command territorial, coercive and economic power outside formal hierarchies; often seek recognition or de facto veto status.

! — INSURGENT / DISRUPTOR
Protest movements, insurgent factions, whistleblower networks, insurgent start-ups. Willing to bear high cost to overturn or delegitimize existing arrangements.

U — UNION / WORKFORCE
Organized labor, professional corps, key guilds. Control the “metabolism” of institutions and economies through work, strikes, slowdowns or professional refusal.

G — ILLEGAL / SHADOW ECONOMY
Organized crime, trafficking networks, illicit markets. Operate outside formal legality but shape incentives, corruption patterns, and violence risks for many other actors.

3. Capital, Narrative & Knowledge Infrastructure

These actors do not always hold formal authority but structure what is financially viable, narratively legitimate, and cognitively thinkable.

$ — FUNDER / CAPITAL PRINCIPAL
Investors, donors, sovereign funds, international financial institutions, major clients. Their exit options and risk appetite define which projects, actors and peace deals are financially sustainable.

B — BUSINESS OPERATOR
Firms that run core activities: utilities, infrastructure operators, critical supply chains, major employers. Their operational choices can stabilize or destabilize whole regions or sectors.

C — CONTRACTOR / VENDOR
Technology providers, implementers, consultancies, private service firms. Deliver specialized capabilities; can entrench dependencies or quietly enable structural reforms.

D — DIGITAL PLATFORM / INFRASTRUCTURE
Social media, messaging apps, cloud providers, payment rails. Their design and moderation choices decide which conflicts become salient, which voices scale, and which grievances remain invisible.

i — MEDIA & INFORMATION GATEKEEPERS
Journalists, broadcasters, investigative outlets, influential commentators. Convert events into narratives; set agendas, amplify scandal or normalize compromise.

S — CIVIL SOCIETY & ADVOCACY NETWORKS
NGOs, social movements, professional associations, faith communities, transnational advocacy networks. Work through information, symbolic, leverage and accountability politics; redefine what counts as a “just” outcome.

X — EPISTEMIC COMMUNITY / EXPERT NETWORK
Scientific panels, policy think tanks, technical standards bodies, professional expert groups. Shape how problems are framed, what counts as evidence, and which solutions are seen as feasible.

H — HUMANITARIAN & SERVICE PROVIDER
Relief agencies, health providers, social services. Maintain social and moral baselines in crises; often hold granular, ground-truth knowledge of how policies translate into lived experience.

Finally, Tacitus includes affected communities (households, identity groups, local constituencies) as explicit nodes when they play a structural role — for example when their withdrawal of consent, votes, labor or tacit compliance can reshape the selectorate or trigger legitimacy crises. In the ontology these appear as Public / Community nodes linked to executives, funders, media and civil society.

By distinguishing these actor families and letting the same institution occupy several roles at once, Tacitus can model conflicts where, for instance, a government is both an Executive and a Funder, a non-state armed group is both an Armed Actor and a de facto Local Authority, or a digital platform is simultaneously Infrastructure, Media and Regulator. That multi-role mapping is essential for realistic conflict intelligence and for designing resolution pathways that respect how power is actually distributed.

06. ONTOLOGY AGENT

DETERMINISTIC PIPELINE, EXPLAINABLE REASONING

AGENTIC ENGINE

The Tacitus Ontology Agent is an AI-augmented reasoning system that commits messy, often emotional reality into the ontology through a deterministic, auditable pipeline. It draws on negotiation theory (Fisher & Ury, Zartman), human needs approaches (Burton, Kelman), and institutional design (Ostrom, Elster) to anchor its inferences.

>> REASONING PIPELINE

  • 1. Structural Reading
    Identify actors, mandates, red lines, and potential veto points. Separate loud actors from structurally decisive ones.
  • 2. Ontology Mapping
    Assign actors, interests, constraints, vectors, and metrics into the Tacitus schema so cases become comparable and cumulative.
  • 3. Scenario Generation
    Generate plausible escalation, stalemate and settlement pathways under different combinations of guarantees, coalitions and procedural moves.
  • 4. Resolution Support
    Translate structural insights into proposals, talking points, and packages tailored to specific mandates and risks: board chairs, mediators, envoys, regulators.

>> BEYOND SEMANTIC SIMILARITY

Most AI tools stop at semantic similarity: clustering texts that sound alike. Tacitus treats this as a signal layer, not the core.

  • > Semantic search finds relevant episodes and arguments.
  • > The Ontology Agent abstracts them into relational patterns: veto configurations, coalition shapes, polarization arcs.
  • > Knowledge is stored as relations and trajectories, enabling agentic systems to reason over who can move what, not just over which sentences resemble each other.

The future of conflict intelligence is graph-aware and agentic: AI systems that understand power, constraints and time, and propose moves that respect both structure and ethics. Tacitus is designed explicitly for that horizon.

07. POLARIZATION & PRE-CONFLICT

TRACKING THE TRAJECTORIES BEFORE THEY BREAK

EARLY WARNING

Polarization is not simply strong disagreement. It becomes dangerous when differences crystallize into identity boundaries, when trust in common procedures erodes, and when each side begins to treat the other as fundamentally illegitimate.

Tacitus treats polarization as a dynamic trajectory: a curve that can steepen, flatten, or bend back, depending on institutions, language, guarantees and gatekeepers.

Signal Layer NARRATIVE

Shifts in language, moral framing and perceived harms reveal where positions are hardening or new grievances are emerging: from “policy error” to “moral betrayal”.

Structural Layer TOPOLOGY

The ontology tracks whether bridging actors still exist, whether common institutions retain legitimacy, and where vetoes are being used as weapons rather than safeguards.

Intervention Layer ACTION

Early warning matters only if it suggests precise moves: whom to convene, which guarantees to offer, which narratives to cool, which procedures to protect or redesign.

The Prism Lab builds on this ontology: it lets practitioners explore how different framings, concessions or institutional choices might bend polarization arcs before conflicts cross critical thresholds.

08. TACITUS LAB

AI, DELIBERATION & THE FUTURE OF DEMOCRATIC DECISION-MAKING

RESEARCH MODE

Tacitus Lab is the experimental arm of the platform. It explores how AI-mediated deliberation, multi-agent architectures and constitutional AI can support better collective decisions without replacing human judgment or responsibility.

Deliberative AI

Inspired by deliberative democracy and Habermasian ideas, we test AI systems that summarize arguments fairly, surface overlapping proposals, and help groups see where they already agree more than they think.

Constitutional & Agentic Systems

Multi-agent set-ups where different AI agents play distinct roles — advocate, critic, mediator, risk officer — constrained by explicit normative rules, not opaque prompts.

Subsidiarity & Freedom

The aim is not technocratic control, but subsidiarity: giving decisions back to the level closest to those affected, equipped with better structural intelligence. Protecting citizens’ freedom means making power legible, not automating it away.

We are prototyping tools where parliaments, boards, missions or movements can:

  • > run “what-if” deliberations across different value frameworks,
  • > test whether proposed rules are robust under stress scenarios,
  • > detect when minority viewpoints are systematically sidelined,
  • > generate agenda-setting questions that widen, rather than narrow, the conversation.

Tacitus Lab sits at the intersection of conflict ontology and democratic theory: engineering systems that make it easier for free and plural societies to reach decisions they can live with — without surrendering moral agency to machines.
