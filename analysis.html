01. PURPOSE

WHAT THE DEEP ANALYSIS VIEW IS FOR

STRUCTURE OVER NOISE

Tacitus is built for places where conflict failure is expensive: boards, ministries, city halls, multilateral missions, and complex project teams. In those environments, one thing is constant: there is far more conflictual communication than any human team can structurally parse. That gap between volume of contention and capacity for resolution is what we call the Resolution Deficit.

The Deep Analysis view shows how Tacitus closes that gap. It exposes the ontology, graph logic, and research backbone that sit underneath the more visual pages of the platform. In practical terms, this is where:

  • > email and document streams are converted into actors, interests, constraints, and vectors
  • > those elements are committed to a graph database rather than left as text embeddings
  • > the system computes leverage points, win-sets, and pathways that respect mandates and vetoes

What follows is the Tacitus Ontology Guide: the structural rules of engagement the engine uses to make sense of conflicts across domains.

02. TACITUS ONTOLOGY GUIDE

STRUCTURAL RULES OF ENGAGEMENT // v3.3

ONTOLOGY STABLE

>> THE TACITUS FRAMEWORK: CYBERNETICS OF POWER & STRUCTURAL REALISM

The Tacitus Engine is not a “smart inbox”. It is a structural analysis framework that synthesizes cybernetics (feedback and control), strategic interaction (game theory), and institutional analysis (selectorate and veto player theories) into a single working ontology.:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1} Instead of asking what the protagonists say they want, Tacitus asks: given the geometry of constraints, what outcomes are even possible?

We model every conflict system — from a nuclear family to a security council — as a circuit of power between:

  • > the Incumbent: the actor or coalition that currently occupies the centre
  • > the Challenger: those seeking to displace, renegotiate, or re-centre power
  • > the Veto Players: actors who can stop a change from the status quo
  • > the Selectorates: those whose backing confers or withdraws legitimacy

By mapping how these nodes depend on one another, Tacitus estimates the system’s Win-Set: the set of agreements that all relevant veto players could accept. Where the Win-Set is empty, escalation is structurally likely. Where it exists, resolution is an algorithmic search problem over that constrained space, not an exercise in wishful thinking.:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

>> CORE STRUCTURAL CONCEPTS

Selectorate Theory. Following Bueno de Mesquita et al., Tacitus distinguishes between the nominal selectorate (everyone who could, in theory, influence leadership), the real selectorate, and the winning coalition whose support is strictly necessary for survival. The smaller the coalition relative to the selectorate, the more politics runs on private goods; the larger it is, the more leaders rely on public goods and broadly defensible outcomes.:contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Veto Player Theory. Building on Tsebelis, Tacitus treats a veto player as any actor whose assent is required to move away from the status quo. Policy rigidity increases with the number of veto players, their ideological distance, and their internal cohesion. That is why the engine cares deeply about who can say “no”, not just who speaks loudest.:contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

>> THE KINETIC / DISCURSIVE DIVIDE

Power operates on two orthogonal planes:

  • > Kinetic Plane — bodies, assets, logistics, territory, violence.
  • > Discursive Plane — narratives, legitimacy, reputations, symbols.

Tacitus explicitly models the feedback loop between the two: discursive dominance lowers the cost of kinetic action; kinetic failures rapidly devalue dominant narratives. In the graph, this shows up as shifts in risk and trust edges after “shocks” (a leak, a strike, a military setback).

>> POWER & RISK METRICS

Power Metric NODE SIZE

Represents the capacity to force or block an outcome. In veto player terms, this is the ability to maintain the status quo against challengers. Power can be formal (votes, legal authority) or structural (control of logistics, capital, or legitimacy).

Risk Metric GLOW INTENSITY

Represents structural vulnerability or cost exposure. High-risk nodes are those whose failure or defection would rewire the entire graph (e.g., a swing regulator, a pivotal coalition partner, a core infrastructure provider).

>> VECTOR ANATOMY (RELATIONSHIPS)

HARD POWER (STRUCTURAL)
  • VETO — The “kill switch”: a legal or functional capacity to halt an action (board vote, court ruling, security council veto). Highest-value edge in the graph.
  • CONFLICT — Kinetic engagement: strikes, blockades, violence. Represents a breakdown in bargaining.
  • FUNDING — Resource provision in principal–agent chains. Introduces control and moral-hazard risks.
  • DEPENDENCY — Existential reliance (energy imports, critical vendors, choke-point infrastructure).
SOFT POWER (INFLUENCE)
  • NARRATIVE — Discursive hegemony: framing, propaganda, leaks, and reputational warfare.
  • PRESSURE — Strikes, protests, boycotts, naming-and-shaming campaigns.
  • ALLIANCE — Formal or informal pacts that aggregate power and often create “tripwires.”
  • PROXY — Indirect intervention through deniable intermediaries to lower kinetic costs and escalation risk.

03. THE ONTOLOGY AGENT

FROM MESSY TEXT TO A STABLE CONFLICT MODEL

DETERMINISTIC PIPELINE

The Tacitus Ontology Agent is the core reasoning module that converts raw, emotionally charged communication into a machine-readable conflict model. It draws on negotiation theory and human needs research (Fisher & Ury on interests vs. positions; Burton and Kelman on needs like security, recognition, and identity; Zartman on “mutually hurting stalemates” and ripeness) to anchor its inferences in well-tested patterns.:contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

>> FOUR-STAGE REASONING PIPELINE

  • 1. IDENTIFICATION
    Extract actors, claims, demands, and explicit positions from emails, memos, minutes, and draft texts. Normalize references (“the board”, “investors”, “legal”) into coherent Actor nodes.
  • 2. INFERENCE
    Infer latent interests behind positions (authorship, fairness, continuity, autonomy, reputational risk). Map them to recurring human needs clusters.
  • 3. STRUCTURING
    Build a constraint matrix (law, budgets, calendars, red lines, coalitions) and a relationship graph (alliances, dependencies, rivalries, mediation channels, trust edges).
  • 4. COMPUTATION
    Run graph algorithms (centrality, bottlenecks, coalition fragility, shortest mediation paths) to locate ZOPA candidates and escalation risks.

>> OUTPUT ARTIFACTS

Each run yields a consistent bundle of artifacts:

  • > Actor registry: roles, mandates, power and risk scores
  • > Interest map: explicit and inferred motivations
  • > Constraint matrix: hard vs. soft constraints, time and law overlays
  • > Relationship graph: alliances, veto edges, trust and mediation lines
  • > ZOPA candidates and resolution scripts, expressed in plain language
  • > Escalation projections: likely fracture points and “bad weather” scenarios

> input: messy narrative

> output: conflict_graph + zopa_pathways

> key_property: deterministic_logic ∧ pluralist_ontology ∧ explainable_outputs

04. STRUCTURE VS SIMILARITY

WHY RESOLUTION LIVES IN A GRAPH DATABASE

CRUCIAL DISTINCTION

Modern AI systems are excellent at semantic similarity: “show me messages like this”. Tacitus uses that capability, but the actual resolution logic runs on a property graph. In that graph, the key question is no longer “what sounds similar?” but: who can move what, at what cost, without triggering which veto?

>> SEMANTIC SEARCH

"Show me all emails similar to: ‘We should delay the vote’."

Useful for clustering themes and surfacing patterns of concern, but blind to structure. It cannot, on its own, tell you who needs to be convinced or what sequence of moves avoids a coalition collapse.

>> GRAPH-BASED REASONING

"Show all paths from Actor A to Actor B that avoid hard vetoes and minimize reputational risk."

Graph queries let Tacitus reason explicitly about paths, bottlenecks, and mediators. The same ontology that powers the Ontology & Conflict Graph view is used here to simulate “what if” sequences and to stress-test proposed corridors of agreement.

(Actor)-[:SEEKS]->(Interest)

(Actor)-[:CONSTRAINED_BY]->(Constraint)

(Actor)-[:IN_RELATIONSHIP {type:"alliance"|"rivalry"|"dependency"}]->(Actor)

(Constraint)-[:LIMITS]->(Solution)

(Interest)-[:COMPATIBLE_WITH]->(Interest)

As new communications arrive, Tacitus updates the graph incrementally, turning the platform into a living conflict model rather than a one-off report.

05. ARCHIVED CASE // GEOPOLITICS

ARCTIC RESOURCE WAR (STYLIZED)

ESCALATION RISK

A geopolitical flashpoint: two states claim the same energy-rich seabed. State A has granted drilling rights to Global Energy Corp; State B has deployed naval assets to blockade the rig. A Defense Pact is treaty-bound to defend State A, creating a classic “tripwire” dynamic. Environmental NGOs and shipping insurers add additional vetoes and reputational risk.

  • NAT-A — Republic of A (Executive; high formal power; alliance-dependent)
  • NAT-B — Federation of B (Executive; revisionist stake in maritime boundary)
  • CORP-X — Global Energy Corp (Contractor / Funder hybrid)
  • MIL-ALL — Defense Pact (Alliance; hard kinetic tripwire)
  • NGO-01 — Eco-Alliance (Civil society; high narrative power)
  • REG-SEA — Maritime Tribunal / Regulator (Regulator; legal veto)

>> STRUCTURAL READING

> escalation_risk: HIGH (alliance tripwire + naval deployments)

06. TACITUS LAB

>> AI, DELIBERATION & THE RESOLUTION DEFICIT

Tacitus sits downstream of a growing body of work on AI-mediated deliberation, constitutional and multi-agent systems, and polarization analysis. Experiments like the “Habermas Machine” show that large language models can help small groups surface overlapping proposals they all judge “fair enough”, without erasing disagreement.:contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

In parallel, constitutional AI research has explored how multiple AI agents, guided by explicit normative rules or “constitutions”, can critique and constrain one another, producing more transparent, auditable outputs.:contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7} Tacitus applies similar ideas, but routes them through a conflict ontology and a graph database: one agent builds the structure, another stress-tests it, a third searches for resolution pathways compatible with law, mandate, and ethics.

Structural, Not Just Semantic

We combine vector search with a graph populated via the Tacitus Ontology: actors, interests, constraints, veto paths, guarantees, trust edges. The same graph powers the Engine, the Prism Lab, and the Ontology Graph.

Mandate-Aware

Every recommendation is conditioned on who you are: CEO, mediator, envoy, regulator. The corridors change depending on your mandate, risk tolerance, and ethical constraints.

Explainable by Construction

Each edge, inferred interest, and proposed step is tied back to specific communications and graph properties. No magic; only auditable reasoning chains.

07. COMMON GROUND & POLARIZATION

FROM POLARIZATION TO DELIBERATION

CONCORDIA DISCORS

Tacitus starts from a simple claim: competing values are not a bug of politics or organizations, they are the default condition. The goal is not to eliminate disagreement, but to make it legible and governable.

Logic of Common Ground INTERSECTIONS

We search not for universal consensus, but for narrow, defensible overlaps: clauses, guarantees, and sequences both sides can live with and publicly defend.

Logic of Deliberation ITERATION

Deliberation is modeled as an iterative update to the conflict graph: misperceptions trimmed, new guarantees and trust edges added, vetoes relaxed or re-anchored.

Polarization FRAGMENTATION

Polarization appears as disconnected subgraphs: clusters that no longer share channels of trust. Tacitus highlights potential “bridge nodes” still heard by both sides.
