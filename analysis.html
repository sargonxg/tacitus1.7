TACITUS ◳ // ONTOLOGY GUIDE v3.3

CONFLICT INTELLIGENCE ONTOLOGY

Tacitus is built for the resolution gap – the distance between recognizing that a situation is dangerous and actually knowing what sequence of moves could de-escalate it. Rather than treating disputes as sentiment or “angry text blobs”, Tacitus constructs a shared graph of power, polarization, and constraints that all products in the stack draw from: the Conflict Intelligence Engine, Prism Lab, and the Ontology Graph.

This page documents how the ontology works: which actors occupy the graph, how relationships are encoded as vectors, how power and risk are measured, and how polarization is monitored as a pre-conflict trajectory.

01. TACITUS ONTOLOGY

STRUCTURAL RULES OF ENGAGEMENT // v3.3

ONTOLOGY STABLE

>> CIRCUITS OF POWER, NOT HEROES AND VILLAINS

The ontology sits at the intersection of systems thinking (Wiener, Easton), strategic interaction (Schelling, Nash, Putnam), and institutional analysis (Tsebelis, Bueno de Mesquita, Ostrom), informed by political and moral philosophy (Arendt, Berlin, Habermas). It assumes that outcomes emerge less from individual psychology than from the geometry of constraints and incentives in which actors move.

Every conflict system is modeled as a circuit between abstract roles. These are the primitives that Tacitus uses when it reads email threads, draft texts, or board minutes.

INC

Incumbents

Actors or coalitions currently occupying the centre: executives, ruling coalitions, dominant factions. Their baseline logic is survival and maintenance, not transformation. In the Engine, they anchor the status-quo scenario against which all proposed changes are evaluated.

CHL

Challengers

Those seeking to shift or displace the status quo: opposition blocs, reform coalitions, insurgent teams, activist investors. They operate under tighter resource constraints but often with higher risk tolerance. Tacitus tracks where their escalation paths intersect with systemic red lines.

V

Veto Players

Following Tsebelis, a veto player is any actor whose consent is required to change course: boards, key ministries, courts, pivotal shareholders, local authorities. The ontology tracks how many exist, how far apart they are, and how cohesive they remain. This drives Tacitus’ assessment of policy stickiness and reform difficulty.

S / W

Selectorates

Building on selectorate theory, Tacitus distinguishes between broad selectorates (those who could influence leadership change) and narrower winning coalitions (whose support is strictly necessary for survival). Changes that shrink the winning coalition or alter its composition are flagged as structurally sensitive moves.

K / D

Kinetic & Discursive Planes

Power operates through bodies and assets (kinetic) and through narrative and legitimacy (discursive). The ontology encodes how shocks in one plane re-price risk in the other – for example, how a protest movement (discursive) increases the cost of repression (kinetic), or how a failed military action collapses a narrative of competence.

Ω

Win-Set Geometry

Inspired by Putnam’s two-level games, Tacitus models the space of outcomes each veto player can accept. The overlap between these spaces – the Win-Set – is where agreements are structurally viable. When that overlap shrinks or vanishes, the system moves into pre-conflict conditions and Prism Lab begins to treat polarization signals as early-warning indicators.

The goal is not to moralize the actors, but to make the structure visible: who must move, who must be reassured, who can credibly guarantee, and which sequences of steps could re-open a viable Win-Set.

02. METRICS LAYER

HOW THE ENGINE SCORES A CONFLICT SYSTEM

METRICS ONLINE

Once actors and relationships are mapped, Tacitus computes a set of shared metrics. These metrics travel with the case: the Conflict Intelligence Engine uses them to surface options, Prism Lab uses them to track polarization, and the Ontology Graph visualizes them as node sizes, glows, and trajectories.

Power Metric

Node size in the Ontology Graph. Aggregates formal authority, control over resources, and veto capacity. Used by the Engine to identify which actors cannot be ignored in any realistic settlement, and by Prism Lab to weight whose rhetoric shifts the system.

Risk Metric

Node glow intensity. Captures exposure to cost and fragility: stretched coalitions, economic dependence, reputational vulnerability, escalation incentives. High-risk nodes often become spoilers or desperate compromise-seekers; Tacitus highlights them either way.

Polarization Gradient

Edge coloring and clustering. Measures how arguments, emotions, and narratives are separating communities over time – not just who disagrees, but how quickly trust is decaying. Prism Lab turns this into polarization trajectories and early-warning dashboards.

Common-Ground Window

A dynamic estimate of propositions that all key veto players could live with, even if none loves them. Computed from overlapping interests, red lines, and audience costs. This is what Tacitus calls the “negotiable corridor” – the concrete target for mediators and boards.

Escalation Sensitivity

For each actor and relationship, Tacitus tracks how likely a small move is to trigger hard reactions (strikes, walk-outs, vetoes, legal challenges). It is fed back into the Engine as a constraint on sequencing: not just what could work, but in which order.

Deliberation Quality Signals

Drawing on deliberative democracy work, Tacitus tags communication streams for symmetry of voice, responsiveness, and reason-giving. Persistent asymmetries are treated as pre-conflict risk factors and can be explored in Tacitus Lab experiments.

03. ACTOR TAXONOMY

WHO OCCUPIES THE GRAPH

NODE TYPES LOADED

The Tacitus ontology is deliberately cross-domain. The same primitives describe a national peace process, a corporate board dispute, a regulatory fight, or an inheritance deadlock. Below are the main actor families Tacitus uses when it rewrites raw communication into a structural map.

Governance & Control

E — Executive. Governments, CEOs, secretariats, office-holders whose primary logic is survival and continuity.

R — Regulator. Courts, commissions, supervisory authorities that translate conflict into rules, thresholds, and precedent.

O — Oversight. Boards, auditors, ombuds, inspectorates – slow to act, but capable of triggering legitimacy crises when they do.

Coercion & Labor

K — Security / Military. Organized coercive apparatus with doctrine, chains of command, and rules of engagement; latent veto players in fragile systems.

! — Insurgent / Disruptor. Protest movements, whistleblowers, insurgent start-ups, opposition factions willing to bear high cost to overturn existing arrangements.

U — Union / Workforce. Organized labor or professional corps, able to halt activity through strikes or slowdowns.

Capital & Supply

$ — Funder. Investors, donors, sovereign funds, major clients whose exit options and risk appetite shape the feasible set of solutions.

C — Contractor. Technology vendors, service providers, consultancies delivering specialized capabilities; often structurally flexible but dependent on contracts and reputation.

Narrative & Information

i — Media. Journalists, platforms, influential commentators who decide which conflicts become visible and how they are framed.

D — Digital Platform / Infrastructure. Operators of key communication and data infrastructures whose design choices silently alter which voices scale and which grievances remain invisible.

Civil & Social Fabric

S — Civil Society. NGOs, professional associations, faith leaders, patient and community groups who can bless or stigmatize a course of action.

P — Local / Mayor. Municipalities, site managers, local leaders who face the first wave of discontent and hold granular knowledge of lived impacts.

Knowledge & Technical Systems

Expert panels, scientific institutes, data teams, standards bodies, and AI systems themselves when they meaningfully constrain options. In Tacitus, these nodes shape what counts as “feasible” and “responsible” in a given domain.

When Tacitus parses a new case, it tries to assign each real-world entity to one or more of these types. This is what allows the same ontology to support corporate, geopolitical, and psychological disputes without rewriting the model for each domain.

04. VECTOR ANATOMY & POLARIZATION

HOW RELATIONSHIPS AND POLARIZATION ARE ENCODED

RELATIONSHIP SCHEMA ACTIVE

Edges in the Tacitus graph are not generic “links”. Each relationship is typed, timed, and scored. This is what lets Tacitus move beyond pure semantic similarity and towards genuine graph-based reasoning.

Hard Power Vectors

Veto. Legal or procedural kill-switches (board votes, court rulings, treaty vetoes).
Dependency. Critical supply, energy, or access relationships.
Conflict / Coercion. Use or threat of force, strikes, sanctions. These edges drive escalation modelling and “what-if” sequences in the Engine.

Soft Power Vectors

Narrative. Framing, propaganda, leaks, agenda-setting.
Pressure. Protests, boycotts, naming-and-shaming, diplomatic signals.
Alliance / Proxy. Coalitions and indirect interventions that expand the scope of a conflict if one node is attacked.

Polarization Trajectories

Prism Lab tracks how these vectors cluster into camps over time: who responds to whom, which arguments harden, which communities stop listening. Polarization is treated as a precursor of conflict – the loss of deliberative capacity before kinetic action appears.

In practical terms, this means Tacitus does not stop at “similar sentences”. It cares about who said what, to whom, under which constraints, and with what downstream effect. Semantic similarity is one signal; graph structure and polarization dynamics are the backbone.

05. ONTOLOGY AGENT & REASONING PIPELINE

FROM RAW TEXT TO STRUCTURED OPTIONS

DETERMINISTIC PIPELINE

Tacitus combines large language models with a deterministic ontology pipeline. The LLMs are used for controlled extraction and paraphrase; the underlying reasoning is enforced by the ontology and graph rules.

1. Ingestion & Normalization

Emails, minutes, position papers, and briefs are cleaned and segmented. Personal identifiers can be minimized or pseudonymized in sensitive contexts. The goal is to move from unstructured noise to canonical “events” and “claims”.

2. Ontology Tagging

The Tacitus Ontology Agent assigns each segment: actors (node types), relationships (vector types), interests, constraints, and early polarization cues. This step is explicit and auditable – the system can show why a passage was tagged as Veto, Dependency, or Narrative.

3. Graph Construction & Metrics

The extracted elements are written into a conflict graph. Power, risk, and polarization metrics are computed on top of that graph, not inside the LLM. This is where Tacitus departs from “pure embeddings”: relationships, veto paths, and coalition geometry become first-class objects.

4. Agentic Monitoring & Scenario Querying

Agentic workflows watch for new documents, detect structural changes (a coalition breaking, a veto actor shifting, a polarization spike), and update the map. Users can then query the case: “What sequences of moves re-open a Win-Set?”, “Who must be reassured for this reform to stick?”, or “What happens if this actor exits?”.

The end result is not a chat transcript, but a living conflict model that can be inspected, challenged, and iterated on – a better foundation for mediation, governance, and institutional learning.

06. TACITUS LAB

DELIBERATIVE AI, SUBSIDIARITY & DEMOCRATIC PRACTICE

EXPERIMENTAL MODULE

Beyond operational conflict work, Tacitus Lab explores how AI can support better democratic decision-making. The reference points here are Habermas’ theory of deliberation, Ostrom’s work on collective governance, and contemporary research on participation and digital publics.

Deliberation-Aware Simulations

Prototype agents that test how proposals fare under different public reasons, not just different vitriol. The aim is to see which arguments could be made legible across camps, and which designs reduce the temptation to bypass debate altogether.

Subsidiarity by Design

Experiments with architectures where local communities, boards, or teams keep control over decisions, while AI supports them with structured evidence, polarization maps, and option trees – rather than replacing their judgment.

Protecting Freedom in High-Conflict Systems

The long-term ambition: tools that make it easier for institutions to act prudently under pressure without collapsing into coercion or paralysis. Tacitus Lab prototypes live alongside Concordia Discors essays on pluralism, dissent, and the ethics of technological mediation.
